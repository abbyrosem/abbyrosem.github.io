Best Practices for Dyslexia-Friendly Digital Content in Publishing and Data Analysis

Making digital content more accessible for people with dyslexia doesn’t have to be complicated; it just takes some thoughtful choices. Whether you are publishing an e-book or designing a data dashboard, there are small things that can make a big difference in how easily everyone can engage with your content.

For Publishers:

The initial step in building accessible reading is to use fonts that are dyslexia-friendly. Fonts like OpenDyslexic or sans-serif fonts like Arial and Verdana are less challenging for most individuals with dyslexia to read (British Dyslexia Association). Offering alternate formats, such as audiobooks or text-to-speech, can also help make the content more accessible to more people. It is also necessary to produce clear layouts with plenty of white space, clear headings, and consistent formatting. These choices reduce cognitive load and allow readers to stay on top without feeling overwhelmed.

For Data Analysts and Designers:

Data can become confused in a rush if visual data is not present. To support users with dyslexia, ensure graphs and charts use high-contrast color schemes and include easy-to-read labels (World Wide Web Consortium, 2018). Avoid cluttering one graphic with too much detail; simpler graphics are much easier to process. Offering interactive elements, such as the ability to adjust font size, background color, or even enable auditory descriptions, can allow users to customize their experience and enhance their understanding of the information. In both data and publishing work, accessibility is less about checking the box and more about making sure your content can be accessed and enjoyed by as many people as possible. These extra steps build an inclusive, thoughtful, and functional digital environment for all.
